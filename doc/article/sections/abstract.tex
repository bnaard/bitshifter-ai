\begin{abstract}
The main hypothesis of this paper is that the current way of constructing neural network architectures using matrix-multiplactions is only one way to do so, and that alternative methods based on bit-level operations can yield competitive performance with lower computational cost. Specifically, implementation of bit-operations could be done avoiding expensive graphics processing unit (GPU) hardware, avoiding technical lock-ins, monopolistic market structures and leveraging standard hardware. Also, environmental aspects, such as energy consumption could be improved. 

This paper proposes a novel neuronal network architecture that leverages bitwise encoding and operations to achieve efficient computation. I present the theoretical foundation of the proposed method, as well as an initial implementation and an initial performance test using the MNIST dataset.

Results demonstrate the potential of this approach in various benchmark tasks, suggesting that \textit{bit-level neural networks can be a viable alternative to traditional architectures}.

Future work will focus on optimizing the proposed architecture and exploring its applicability to more complex tasks and datasets, specifically the applicability to large-language models (LLMs) and other state-of-the-art architectures.

\end{abstract}
